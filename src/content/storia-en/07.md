# Afterword

The stories told in books all have an ending, and the one we have recounted in these pages — spanning more than 150 years — is now drawing to a close. We were reluctant, however, to leave it without a fitting conclusion, and before publishing we decided to wait for some sign that the dreadful period we have all just lived through was beginning to move towards its end.

Touching wood, it does indeed seem that things are heading in that direction. The vaccines have arrived, and a great many people — enough to form, thankfully, a very substantial majority — have taken up the invitation to be vaccinated, even if the reasons that led them to do so may have been the most varied. Life is returning, little by little, to something resembling normality. Schools have reopened, people have gone back to their workplaces, it is possible to travel without excessive difficulty, and restaurants and other public venues have resumed operating at full capacity.

Even if everything seems to be tending back towards regularity and routine, nobody believes things will truly be as they were before. We too, in our small way, have noticed that much has changed. The pandemic has left its mark, and we are still trying to take stock of the situation and understand where we stand.

Some people are no longer among us. Others, though struck down by Covid, managed to pull through without excessive harm. Regardless of their state of health, still others decided to step back — temporarily or for good, we shall see — from their commitment to the band. The gaps are visible, even if the solid core has held: the Filarmonica has undoubtedly withstood the shock and can now count the pandemic among the hardships it has managed to overcome.

There are also more than a few positive signs. First of all, rehearsals have resumed on a regular basis — a vitally important development that allows us to reconnect, compare notes and share a very significant part of what band life is all about. Before long we shall be back rehearsing in our natural home, with heartfelt thanks to the friends who hosted us during this period. There have been a few small outings, the first rounds of applause have been earned, and plans are beginning to take shape, in the hope that they will not have to be forcibly abandoned.

The School of Music has also started up again, and new enrolments have been more than satisfactory in number. As was foreseeable and natural, there has been an influx of new members: if some have left, others have joined, the cycle has started up again and continuity is assured. Interestingly, those joining are not only students of the School and members of the Youth Band, but also adults who played elsewhere or who have picked up their instrument again after years — even decades — of inactivity.

There is also a notable number of adult enrolments in the music school itself: people who have not played for years, people who did play but wish to learn a new instrument, or people who have never played at all and would like to find out what it feels like.

This drawing closer to music (and ultimately to the Society) on the part of mature individuals is perhaps the most unexpected development of all, and one we shall need to reflect on in the near future in order to understand it, channel it, and encourage it. Perhaps it is a side effect of the pandemic: after being forced to interact only at a distance, to do things virtually, to see others through a computer screen, there has been an explosion of appetite for reality, for participation, for human interaction — and making music together is one of the finest ways to meet and fulfil that need.

This experience has certainly changed us and helped us grow. Perhaps it has also taught us something, and even if the price paid is, from every point of view, excessive, what has been gained in return is far from negligible.

In short: if the stories told in books all have an ending, the stories lived out in the daily fabric of real life go on — even if the protagonists change from time to time. And the story of our Filarmonica is no exception. The pandemic has passed, life goes on, and if until now all our attention had been devoted to the first 150 years of its history, from here on all our interest is focused on what comes _... and counting_.

As we send this work to press, the wish we extend to those who have read and followed us is to meet again in ten, twenty or more years' time to tell more stories about the _banda de Turiàc_.
