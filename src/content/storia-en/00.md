# Preface

> When men make plans, the gods laugh.

We wish to open the book you are about to read with this aphorism, which some believe to be a Greek saying, others a Hebrew proverb (with the second part of the sentence in the singular), and which, in any case, everyone can find quoted in the opening titles of *Amnesia* by G. Salvatores. No sentence could have proved more prophetic and apt. And now we will explain why.

Quite some time ago, someone noticed that the 150th anniversary of the founding of what everyone here calls *la banda de Turiàc* — but which, in official documents and reports, is referred to as the Società Filarmonica di Turriaco — was approaching. Band or society as it may be, 150 years of activity undoubtedly represented an important milestone that deserved to be properly celebrated. Plaques, awards, speeches and concerts are all well and good, but these are things we have seen time and again; for this occasion, something special was called for.

The project began rather cautiously with the idea of a photo book. In previous years there had already been exhibitions of images from the past, but a book would represent something more concrete and lasting: photographs and illustrations would always be available and would make an interesting keepsake for friends, guests and visitors.

The idea did not, however, fully convince everyone. Books of that kind already existed in abundance, and a collection of photographs did not seem sufficient to celebrate such an important anniversary. Someone raised the stakes: *Why don't we write a book about the history of the band and include the photographs?* The immediate objection was: *And who is going to write it?* The counter-objection was that, all things considered, writing a book is not an impossible undertaking, and that some of us already had some experience in the matter.

It did not take long for the book project to start taking shape. The Board of Directors gave its approval. A working group was set up, tasked with collecting photographic documentation and drafting the text. A group coordinator was appointed, meetings began, first drafts were circulated — in short, everything seemed to be going smoothly when… Covid arrived! The world we knew, the one we were accustomed to, with its certainties and its routines, suddenly vanished.

The last "normal" event in which the band took part was the traditional Christmas Concert in December 2019. Then everyone learned the meaning of the word *lockdown*. Rehearsals were suspended, as were lessons at the School of Music and already-scheduled concerts. On Easter Sunday, instead of the usual procession, the band's greetings to the town were broadcast through the loudspeakers mounted on the Civil Protection vehicles.

In summer, a concert was held in the square, but it felt unnatural. Spectators (few and monitored) seated at a safe distance, each wearing their mask. Band members forced to take the stage also masked, and to play with unusual gaps between them. To allow a wider audience to participate, the event was streamed on Facebook (and this, it must be said, was very much appreciated). In autumn, thanks to the generosity of a couple of friends who made available a much larger venue than usual, rehearsals could resume in preparation for the Christmas Concert — but then the second wave of the pandemic arrived, right on cue.

Despite all these difficulties and setbacks, work on the book managed to press on. 2020 passed, and with it the 150th anniversary. There were no celebrations, of course, but no matter. If the 150th could not be celebrated, we would celebrate the 151st; if that too proved impossible, we would celebrate the 152nd. What matters is knowing that we are still together, that the instruments are always there, even if playing them alone does not have the same flavour as playing them in a group. The band and the Society are still very much alive, and this publication is direct proof of that.

This is a book about our history — a history told by us, as it was passed down to us, as we remember it, and as we wish to pass it on to those who will come after us. It may be a book with a few flaws, and in some respects it may even seem a little naïve, but it is something entirely ours, conceived, written and produced without recourse to intermediaries.

We undertook this work fundamentally for ourselves, to remind us of what we have been and to help us be, as best we can, what we are. But the book was also written with those close to us in mind: for the friends and relatives of the old band members who are no longer with us, for the inhabitants of this town who have known us all their lives and who have followed us, showing their warmth and affection on every occasion, for the colleagues from other bands with whom we have played, for the maestros, singers and choirs we have come into contact with over all these years.

Finally, the book was written also with those in mind who have never heard of us, who do not yet know us and know nothing of the small and great things that have happened here — and who, from reading these pages, will be helped to discover similarities and differences with their own realities.

What we recount we have drawn from the Society's archive (still largely unexplored), from personal memories, and from what we have read in larger books. Not being historians — either by profession or by inclination — and having no claim to scholarly rigour, we will avoid making painstaking bibliographical references; but we cannot fail to cite, for general background information, two fundamental works about our town: the *Storia di Turriaco* by Mario Furioso and the *Note storiche nella ricorrenza dei 150 anni del Comune di Turriaco* by Vittorio Alberto Spanghero, from which we have freely drawn.

In particular, we wish to thank Vittorio for the generosity with which he made available his time, his archive and his knowledge. Alongside these works we wish to mention the manuscript *Nel buio del tempo* by Silvio Gualtiero Cosolo, which we were generously permitted to read and use, and to express our gratitude to Rinaldo Baldo who, with his meticulous photographic archive, made it easier to document the last decade of the band's life.

We also wish to thank the *Circolo Culturale e Ricreativo don Eugenio Brandl*, which allowed us to use some splendid images from its annual calendars, and our friend Fabio Bergamasco, who gave us invaluable help in designing the cover of this volume.

A heartfelt thank-you, finally, to Maestro Maurizio Zaccaria, who sent us the long and affectionate letter which — though trimmed of mention of some events already covered in the text — we have published in the preceding pages.

It may, however, come as a surprise not to find an author's name on the title page. From the very beginning we decided there would be no *I*, but that the work would be done by *us*. Every single word, regardless of who may have written it, was read, discussed and commented upon within the working group, and every single decision — on topics to cover, how to present them, which photographs to include, which quotations to insert, and so on — was taken by common agreement.

In the hope that what follows may reawaken your memories, rekindle your interest, or simply arouse your curiosity, we wish you happy reading.

**NOTE.** When this work was already complete, Silvio Gualtiero Cosolo passed away suddenly — former president of the Filarmonica and its living historical memory. His loss leaves in each of us a void that is hard to fill. With his three-quarters of a century of service to the band, Silvio played a fundamental role in the history we are about to tell — a history we chose to leave unchanged, even where Silvio is spoken of in the present tense. It is the way we have chosen to remember him and to make him feel still among us.
